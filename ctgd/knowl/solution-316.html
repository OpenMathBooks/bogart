Solution 196.1

\((x+y)^n=\sum_{i=0}^\infty\binom{n}{i}x^i\text{.}\) The proof consists of writing \((x+y)=y(\frac{x}{y}+1)\) and applying the ProblemÂ 195 when \(n\) is negative. When \(n\) is positive, we recall that \(\binom{n}{k}\) is zero when \(k>n\text{,}\) so replacing the upper limit of \(n\) in the standard version of the binomial theorem by \(\infty\) doesn't change the value of the sum. When \(n=-3\) we get \((x+y)^n = \sum_{i=0}^\infty \binom{-3}{i}x^iy^{-3-i} = \sum_{i=0}^\infty (-1)^i \binom{3+i-1}{i}x^iy^{-3-i}\text{.}\) Expanding our binomial coefficients gives \((x+y)^{-3} = \binom{2}{0}x^0y^{-3} -\binom{3}{1}x^1y^{-4} + \binom{4}{2}x^2y^{-5}-\cdots.\) The surprise is that we get an infinite series in positive and negative powers of variables. In order to limit ourselves to infinite series with nonnegative exponents, we do not pursue this idea further.
