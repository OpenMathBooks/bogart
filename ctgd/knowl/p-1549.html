You see that the graph has two directed cycles, the rather trivial one with vertex 4 pointing to itself, and the nontrivial one with vertex 1 pointing to vertex 2 pointing to vertex 3 which points back to vertex 1. A permutation is called a cycle if its digraph consists of exactly one cycle. Thus \(\begin{pmatrix}1\amp 2\amp 3\cr2\amp 3\amp 1 \end{pmatrix}\) is a cycle but \(\begin{pmatrix}1\amp 2\amp 3\amp 4\cr2\amp 3\amp 1\amp 4 \end{pmatrix}\) is not a cycle by our definition. We write \((1\ 2\ 3)\) or \((2\ 3\ 1)\) or \((3\ 1\ 2)\) to stand for the cycle \(\sigma =\begin{pmatrix}1\amp 2\amp 3\cr2\amp 3\amp 1 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\)
