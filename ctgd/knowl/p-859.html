To be precise, we define \(x^{\underline{j}}\) to be \(x(x-1)\cdots (x-j+1)\text{.}\) At first glance it looks like we could express \(x^{\underline{j}}\) in terms of powers of \(x\) by simply substituting \(x\) for \(n\) in the equation \(\sum_{j=0}^n n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j) = n^k\text{.}\) However this gives us \(\sum_{j=0}^x x^{\underline{j}}S(k,j) = x^k\text{,}\) and we have never defined what we mean by a sum whose upper limit is a variable \(x\text{.}\) Thus we need to examine the equation \(\sum_{j=0}^n n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j) = n^k\) to see if we can replace the \(n\) that is the upper limit of the sum with something else. Notice that \(S(k,j)=0\) when \(j>k\text{.}\) This means that if \(k\le j\text{,}\) then \(\sum_{j=0}^n n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j)=\sum_{j=0}^k n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j)\text{.}\) Notice also that \(n^{\underline{j}}= n(n-1)\cdots(n-j+1)\) is zero when \(j>n\) because one of its factors is zero then. This implies that if \(k>j\text{,}\) then \(\sum_{j=0}^n n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j)=\sum_{j=0}^k n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j)\text{.}\) Therefore, regardless of the relative size of \(k\) and \(n\text{,}\) we have that \(\sum_{j=0}^n n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j)=\sum_{j=0}^k n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j)\text{.}\) Therefore

\begin{equation} \sum_{j=0}^k n^{\underline{j}}S(k,j)=n^k. \tag{3.1} \end{equation} in-context
