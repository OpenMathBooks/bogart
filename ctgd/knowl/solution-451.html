Solution266.1

It will always be the case that all the entries of a row or column below and to the left of the lines in a group table are different. If two entries of the row labelled by \(\sigma\) were equal, that would mean \(\sigma\circ\varphi_1 = \sigma\circ\varphi_2\) for two different elements \(\varphi_1\) and \(\varphi_2\text{.}\) But if we multiply both sides of the equation by \(\sigma^{-1}\) we get \(\sigma^{-1}(\sigma\circ\varphi_1) = \sigma^{-1}(\sigma\circ\varphi_2)\text{,}\) and by using the associative law and the identity property, we get \(\varphi_1=\varphi_2\text{,}\) a contradiction. The same sort of argument (with \(\sigma\) on the right) works for columns.
