You showed in Problem 152 how to get each power of \(x\) in terms of the falling factorial powers \(x^{\underline{j}}\text{.}\) Therefore every polynomial in \(x\) is expressible in terms of a sum of numerical multiples of falling factorial powers. Using the language of linear algebra, we say that the ordinary powers of \(x\) and the falling factorial powers of \(x\) each form a basis for the “space” of polynomials, and that the numbers \(S(k,n)\) are “change of basis coefficients.” If you are not familiar with linear algebra, a basis for the space of polynomials 3  is a set of polynomials such that each polynomial, whether in that set or not, can be expressed in one and only one way as a sum of numerical multiples of polynomials in the set.
