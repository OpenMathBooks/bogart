Solution 364.1

We guess the formula

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{i(i+1)} = \frac{n}{n+1}. \end{equation*}

When \(n=1\) this formula says that \(\frac{1}{1\cdot2}=\frac{1}{1\cdot 2}\text{,}\) so our formula holds when \(n=1\text{.}\) Now assume that \(k>1\) and that our formula holds when \(n=k-1\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \frac{1}{i(i+1)}= \frac{k-1}{k}. \end{equation*}

Adding \(\frac{1}{k(k+1)}\) to both sides of this equation gives us

\begin{align*} \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \frac{1}{i(i+1)}+\frac{1}{k(k+1)} =\amp \frac{k-1}{k}+\frac{1}{k(k+1)}\\ \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{1}{i(i+1)} =\amp \frac{(k-1)(k+1)}{k(k+1)}+\frac{1}{k(k+1)}\\ =\amp \frac{k^2 -1 +1}{k(k+1)}\\ =\amp \frac{k}{k+1}. \end{align*}

Thus whenever our formula is true with \(n=k-1\text{,}\) it is true with \(n=k\) as well. Therefore by the principle of mathematical induction, our formula is true for all positive integers.
