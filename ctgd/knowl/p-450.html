We wish to prove that \(\binom{n}{i} =\frac{n!}{i!(n-i)!}\text{.}\) We note that since \(\binom{n}{0}=1\) and \(\binom{n}{n} =1\) are in agreement with this formula, we only have to consider the cases in which \(0\lt i\lt n\text{,}\) which by the way, requires that \(n\ge 2\text{.}\) We will prove that our formula holds by induction on \(n\) for \(n\ge 2\text{.}\) If \(n=2\text{,}\) the only \(i\) we need to consider is \(i=1\text{,}\) and we know that \(\binom{2}{1}=2\text{,}\) the number of one-element subsets of a two-element set. But \(\frac{2!}{1!(2-1)!}\) is 2 also, so our formula holds when \(n=2\text{.}\) Now suppose our formula holds when \(n=k-1\text{,}\) so that for every \(i\) with \(0\lt i\lt n-1\text{,}\) \(\binom{k-1}{i} = \frac{(k-1)!}{i!(k-1-i)!}\text{.}\) Then by the Pascal Equation

\begin{align*} \binom{k}{i} =\amp \binom{k-1}{i-1}+\binom{k-1}{i}\\ =\amp \frac{(k-1)!}{(i-1)!(k-1-i+1)!} + \frac{(k-1)!}{i!(k-1-i)!}\\ =\amp \frac{(k-1)!i+(k-1)!(k-i)}{i!(k-i)!} \ =\ \frac{k!}{i!(k-i)!}. \end{align*}
