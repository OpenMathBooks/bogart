We prove the formula for \(f\) by induction on \(n\text{.}\) If \(n=0\text{,}\) then \(n(n+1)/2=0\) which is what we were given. Now assume that \(f(k-1)= (k-1)k/2\text{.}\) Then \(f(k)= k+f(k-1)= k+(k-1)k/2=(k^2+2k-k)/2=k(k+1)/2\text{.}\) Therefore the truth of the formula for \(n=k-1\) implies its truth for \(n=k\text{,}\) and thus by the principle of mathematical induction, the formula for \(f\) holds for all nonnegative integers \(n\text{.}\)
