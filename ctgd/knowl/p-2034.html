We prove our result by induction on \(m\text{.}\) If \(m=1\text{,}\) then either \(n = 1\) and we can choose \(q = 1\) and \(r = 0\text{,}\) or \(n \gt 1\) and we can choose \(q = 0\) and \(r = 1\text{.}\) Furthermore, if \(m = 1\) and \(n = 1\text{,}\) then \(r\) must equal zero (to be less than 1), and so \(q\) must equal 1. In \(m=1\) and \(n \gt 1\text{,}\) then we must choose \(q = 0\) (because otherwise \(nq+r\) would have to be bigger than \(m\)) and therefore must choose \(r = m\text{.}\) Therefore Euclid's theorem is true when \(m = 1\text{.}\)
