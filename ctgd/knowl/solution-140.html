Solution78.1

We define \(\sum_{i=1}^1a_i = a_1\) and for \(n>1\text{,}\) \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}a_i\right) +a_n\text{.}\) When \(n=1\text{,}\) \(b\sum_{i=1}^1a_i =ba_1\) by the base step of our inductive definition. Assume that \(k>1\) and \(b\sum_{i=1}^{k-1}a_i=\sum_{i=1}^{k-1}ba_i\text{.}\) Now we can write

\begin{equation*} b\sum_{i=1}^k a_i\!=\! b\left[\left(\sum_{i=1}^{k-1}a_i\right)+a_k\right] \!=\! \left(b\sum_{i=1}^{k-1}a_i\right) +ba_k \!=\! \left(\sum_{i=1}^{k-1}ba_i\right) + ba_k \!=\! \sum_{i=1}^k ba_i, \end{equation*}

where the last step is justified by the inductive step of our inductive definition with \(a_i\) replaced by \(ba_i\text{.}\) Thus the truth of our statement for \(k-1\) implies its truth for \(i=k\text{,}\) and therefore by the principle of mathematical induction, for all positive integers \(n\text{,}\) \(b\sum_{i=1}^na_i= \sum_{i=1}^nba_i\text{.}\)
