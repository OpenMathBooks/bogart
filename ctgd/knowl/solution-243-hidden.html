We know that

\begin{equation} (x+1)^n=\sum_{i=0}^n \binom{n}{i}x^{i}\tag{3.2} \end{equation}

from the binomial theorem. (In the way we stated the binomial theorem, instead of \(\binom{n}{i}x^i\) we would have gotten \(\binom{n}{i}x^{n-i}\text{.}\) There are two ways to fix this. One is to observe that the coefficient of \(x^i\) in that expansion is \(\binom{n}{n-i}\text{,}\) which equals \(\binom{n}{i}\text{.}\) The other is to observe that when we expand \((1+x)^n\) according to the binomial theorem we get exactly what we wrote on the right hand side in EquationÂ (3.2).) Therefore every power of \(x+1\) is expressible in terms of powers of \(x\text{.}\)

How do we express powers of \(x\) in terms of powers of \(x+1\text{?}\) Some experimentation would help us guess how to do so; however there is a really nice trick that also isn't hard to see. Namely, we can write

\begin{align*} x^n =(x+1-1)^n= [(x+1) -1]^n =\amp \sum_{i=0}^n \binom{n}{i}(x+1)^{n-i}(-1)^i\\ =\amp \sum_{i=0}^n \binom{n}{i}(x+1)^i(-1)^{n-i} \end{align*}

This means that every power of \(x\) is expressible in terms of powers of \(x+1\) and the change of basis coefficients to express powers of \(x\) in terms of powers of \(x+1\) are \((-1)^{n-i}\binom{n}{i}\) while the change of basis coefficients used to express powers of \(x+1\) in terms of powers of \(x\) are \(\binom{n}{i}\text{.}\)
