Note that by the formula, \(a_{n-1}=ba_{n-2} +d\text{.}\) Substituting this into the original equation for \(a_n\) gives \(a_n=b^2a_{n-2}+bd +d\text{.}\) Repeating this kind of substitution gives us \(a_n=b^3a_{n-3} +b^2d +bd +d\text{.}\) This suggests that \(a_n = a_0b^n +\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}db^i\text{.}\) We would guess the same formula by writing out the first few values of \(a_i\text{,}\) namely, \(a_0\text{,}\) \(a_0b+d\text{,}\) \(a_0b^2+db+d\text{,}\) \(a_0b^3+b^2d+bd+d\text{,}\) and so on. We prove our general formula by induction on \(n\text{.}\) It is clearly true when \(n=0\) as there are no terms in the sum and \(b^0=1\text{.}\) If we assume the formula is true when \(n=k-1\text{,}\) we may write

\begin{align*} a_k =\amp ba_{k-1}+d\\ =\amp b(a_0b^{k-1}+ b\left(\sum_{i=0}^{n-2}db^i\right) +d\\ =\amp a_0b^n +\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}db^i \end{align*}

Thus the truth of our formula for \(n=k-1\) implies its truth for \(n=k\) and therefore by the principle of mathematical induction, it is true for all nonnegative integers \(n\text{.}\)

We can give a more compact expression for the sum \(\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}db^i=d\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}b^i\text{.}\) Recall from algebra that \((1+x)(1-x)= 1-x^2\text{,}\) \((1+x+x^2)(1-x) = 1-x^3\text{,}\) and in general \((1+x+x^2+\cdots x^{n-1}))(1-x) = 1-x^n\text{.}\) If you do not recall this formula, you can prove it by induction, or observe that

\begin{align*} \amp (1+x+x^2+\cdots x^{n-1}))(1-x)\\ =\amp (1+x+x^2+\cdots x^{n-1})\cdot 1-(1+x+x^2+\cdots x^{n-1})\cdot x\\ =\amp 1+x+x^2+\cdots x^{n-1}-(x+x^2+\cdots+x^n)\rangle =\rangle 1-x^n. \end{align*}

Dividing the first and last terms by \(1-x\) gives us

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}x^i=\frac{1-x^n}{1-x}. \end{equation*}

Using this in our formula for \(a_n\) gives us \(a_n =a_ob^n+d\frac{1-b^n}{1-b}.\) This is valid except in the case \(b=1\) (in our computation with \(x\) above, we would be dividing by 0.) If \(b=1\) we get \(a_n =a_0 +nd\) for the sum.
