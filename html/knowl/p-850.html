We know that

\begin{equation} (x+1)^n=\sum_{i=0}^n \binom{n}{i}x^{i} \tag{3.2} \end{equation}

from the binomial theorem. (In the way we stated the binomial theorem, instead of \(\binom{n}{i}x^i\) we would have gotten \(\binom{n}{i}x^{n-i}\text{.}\) There are two ways to fix this. One is to observe that the coefficient of \(x^i\) in that expansion is \(\binom{n}{n-i}\text{,}\) which equals \(\binom{n}{i}\text{.}\) The other is to observe that when we expand \((1+x)^n\) according to the binomial theorem we get exactly what we wrote on the right hand side in EquationÂ (3.2).) Therefore every power of \(x+1\) is expressible in terms of powers of \(x\text{.}\)
