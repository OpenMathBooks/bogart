We prove the binomial theorem by induction on \(n\text{.}\) When \(n=0\text{,}\) \((x+y)^n=(x+y)^0=1=\sum_{i=0}^0 \binom{n}{i}x^{0-i}y^i\) since that last summation consists of the one term \(\binom{0}{0}x^0y^0\text{.}\)

Now suppose that when \(n=k-1\text{,}\) \((x+y)^n=\sum_{i=0}^n \binom{n}{i}x^{n-i}y^i.\) This gives us

\begin{align*} (x+y)^k =\amp (x+y)(x+y)^{k-1}=(x+y)\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}\binom{k-1}{i}x^{k-1-i}y^i\\ =\amp \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}\binom{k-1}{i}x^{k-i}y^i+\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \binom{k-1}{i}x^{k-1-i}y^{i+1}\\ =\amp \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}\binom{k-1}{i}x^{k-i}y^i+\sum_{i=1}^{k} \binom{k-1}{i-1}x^{k-i}y^{i}\\ =\amp x^k+\left(\sum_{i=1}^{k-1}\binom{k-1}{i}x^{k-i}y^i+\binom{k-1}{i-1}x^{k-i}y^i\right) +y^k\\ =\amp x^k+\left(\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \binom{k}{i}x^{k-i}y^i\right) +y^k\ =\ \sum_{i=0}^k \binom{k}{i}x^{k-i}y^i. \end{align*}

Thus the truth of the binomial theorem for \(n=k-1\) implies its truth for \(n=k\text{.}\) Then by the principle of mathematical induction, the binomial theorem must be true for all integers \(n\ge 0\text{.}\)
