We will use induction on \(n\) and part (a) of this problem to prove that \(a^{mn}=(a^m)^n\text{.}\) First, when \(n=0\) the left and right hand sides of the equation are both 1, so \(a^{mn}=(a^m)^n\) holds when \(n=0\text{.}\) Now assume that \(a^{m(k-1)} =(a^m)^{k-1}\text{.}\) This may be rewritten as \(a^{mk-m}=(a^m)^{k-1}\) Multiply both sides by \(a^m\) and apply part (a) of the problem and then the inductive definition (with \(a^m\) replacing \(a\)) to get

\begin{align*} a^{mk-m}a^m =\amp (a^m)^{k-1}a^m\\ a^{mk} =\amp (a^m)^{k-1}a^m\\ a^{mk} =\amp (a^m)^k. \end{align*} in-context
