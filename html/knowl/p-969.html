The number of partitions of \(k\) into \(n\) parts is equal to the number of partitions of \(k+\binom{n}{2}\) into n distinct parts. The bijection from partitions of \(k\) with \(n\) parts to partitions of \(k+\binom{n}{2}\) with \(n\) distinct parts that proves this is the one that takes a partition \(\lambda_n\lambda_{n-1}\cdots\lambda_1\) of \(k\) with \(\lambda_i>\lambda_{i+1}\) and adds \(i-1\) to \(\lambda_i\) to get \(\lambda'_i\text{.}\) Then \(\lambda'\) is a partition into distinct parts, and the number it partitions is \(k+1+2+\cdots+n-1=k+\binom{n}{2}\text{.}\) The proof that it is a bijection is the fact that subtracting \(n-i\) from the \(i\)\/th part of a partition of \(k\) into distinct parts yields a partition of \(k\text{,}\) because part \(i+j\) is at least \(j\) smaller than part \(i\text{.}\)
